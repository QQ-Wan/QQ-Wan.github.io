I study how attention shapes learning across development. At its core, attention is how minds cope with an overwhelming flood of information — external and internal — by selecting what matters in a given moment. What we attend to determines what we encode, and what we encode becomes the foundation of what we know.
My graduate work examined this process in early childhood through tightly controlled experiments, such as [how working memory constrains attentional selectivity during category learning](https://doi.org/10.1037/xge0001848), and [how developmental differences in attention shape children's exploration behavior](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09567976241258146). These studies revealed how even small shifts in what young learners attend to can reshape what they come to understand about the world.
My current work takes a different approach. Rather than isolating attention in simplified tasks, I study the full learning cycle — from attention to memory — as it unfolds in navigable, naturalistic environments using immersive virtual reality and neuroimaging. I've come to embrace the noise and variance of real-world data as signal rather than nuisance. A central part of human development, I think, is learning to coordinate across modalities, tasks, and goals, and understanding that coordination requires studying minds in contexts closer to the ones they actually evolved to handle.

I completed my Ph.D. in Psychology at The Ohio State University, advised by [Vladimir Sloutsky](https://psychology.osu.edu/people/sloutsky.1). I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at UC Davis, working with [Charan Ranganath](https://psychology.ucdavis.edu/people/charan) and [Joy Geng](https://psychology.ucdavis.edu/people/jgeng).
